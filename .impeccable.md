# Design Context — Steam Taste Lens

## Users
Steam players with substantial libraries (20+ games, often 1000+ for power users) who want to understand their own playing patterns better. Late-night, post-session, "what am I really like as a player" mood. Self-curious, not anxious. They want a tool that genuinely knows games and treats them like a person, not a session ID.

## Brand Personality
Three words: **honest, gamer-native, considerate**.

The product helps players see themselves clearly — what they actually love, what they own and never started, what they might love next. It's a quiet companion that reads your library like a friend who actually plays, not a recommender trying to maximize wallet share.

Emotional goal: the user feels **seen and understood**, like a magazine feature dedicated to them as a player. Warm, slightly playful, never preachy or mocking.

## Aesthetic Direction
**Retro arcade meets early-90s gamer magazine**, executed with modern editorial craft.

- Pixel display type and CRT-phosphor accents borrowed from late-80s / early-90s gaming culture — a love letter to where players come from
- Asymmetric layout, varied type scale, intentional negative space (the editorial half)
- NOT cliché 8-bit kawaii. NOT a fake game UI. NOT a nostalgic gimmick skin.
- Reference physical objects: a 1991 game cartridge paper insert printed on cream stock; a worn-edge gaming magazine "PLAYER PROFILE" spread; an arcade cabinet sticker

**Theme**: dark, warm-tinted. Charcoal / oxblood base with phosphor amber accent. Body text on warm bone, not cool gray. Late-night reading energy.

**Anti-references** (visual fingerprints to actively avoid — purely about look, not stance):
- SaaS dashboard look (Linear / Vercel admin) — single blue accent, glass cards, uniform spacing
- Glassmorphism, neon glow, purple-blue gradients (AI design tells)
- Inter / DM Sans / Plex / Geist / Outfit family (AI reflex fonts)
- Monospace-everywhere lazy "technical" signal
- Cliché chunky 8-bit pixel art that screams "I am pretending to be a video game"

## Design Principles
1. **Editorial over dashboard** — each page is a magazine spread with hierarchy, ornament, and rhythm. The user's data IS the editorial content; let it breathe.
2. **Pixel grit, modern craft** — pixel display fonts and ornamental glyphs nod to gaming culture; spacing, hierarchy, color discipline, and typography pairing come from contemporary editorial design.
3. **Warmth, not coolness** — warm dark surfaces + amber phosphor accent. The tool feels handmade by someone who plays, not assembled from a component library. Color is rare and meaningful (60-30-10).
4. **Numbers as ornament** — playtimes, percentages, library counts ARE the story. Render them monumentally in display type, treat them as headlines, not table cells.
5. **Refuse the AI-template fingerprint** — no `border-left:` stripes, no gradient text, no glassmorphism, no Inter-family fonts. Every decorative move must be intentional and project-specific.

## Accessibility
- WCAG AA contrast minimum for body text (verified via OKLCH lightness diff)
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` — pixel / CRT scanline effects must have a no-motion fallback
- Pixel display fonts only at h1 / h2 and ornamental numbers (sizes ≥ 28px); never for body or labels (illegibility risk)
- All interactive elements have non-color affordances (border, outline, weight change) on top of color
